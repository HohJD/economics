Economics made visual

Learn how demand and supply shape prices

This lesson is now split into short slides so the story feels easier to follow: first the demand curve, then the supply curve, and finally the market curve where both meet.

Slide 1 · Demand

Start with the demand curve

Demand shows how much buyers want at each price. When the price rises, quantity demanded usually falls, so we move along the same curve.

  • Price changes cause movement along the demand curve.
  • The curve itself stays in place unless something else changes.
  • Think: “higher price → less quantity demanded.”

Slide 2 · Supply

Then look at the supply curve

Supply shows how much sellers are willing to offer at each price. When the price rises, quantity supplied usually rises too, so we move along the supply curve.

  • Price changes also cause movement along the supply curve.
  • The curve itself does not move unless a non-price factor changes.
  • Think: “higher price → more quantity supplied.”
Supply curve Price ↑ → Qs ↑

At a higher price, sellers are willing to offer more. That is a movement along the supply curve.

Slide 3 · Market

Now bring both curves together

This is the full market picture. The demand curve and the supply curve meet at equilibrium, where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied.

  • When the price is below equilibrium, there is a shortage.
  • When the price is above equilibrium, there is a surplus.
  • Shifts happen when something other than price changes.

Slide 4 · Curve shifts

Now watch the whole curve move

This is the key difference: when something other than price changes, the whole curve shifts — the old curve is dashed, the new one is solid — and the market settles at a new equilibrium.

18
  • Demand shifts when tastes, income, substitutes, or expectations change.
  • Supply shifts when technology, input costs, or taxes change.
  • After a shift, the market finds a new equilibrium price and quantity.
Demand shifts right when buyers want more at every price.

Movement along the curve

Only the good’s own price changes. The point moves along the same curve.

Shift of the curve

Something else changes, like income, tastes, technology, or costs. The entire curve moves.
